When Borders Kill: Pushbacks, Deterrence, and Migrant Mortality along the Balkan Route

Abstract. This paper studies pushbacks—defined as illegal cross-border expulsions conducted by border authorities—as a case of institutionalized deterrence along the Western Balkan Route. I ask whether deterrence-oriented border policies increase the occurrence of migrant deaths. Using a monthly region-level (NUTS-3) panel across twelve Eastern European countries from 2017 to 2024, I combine two-way fixed effects, Poisson, and counterfactual estimators to identify the causal effect of pushbacks on migrant fatalities. The findings show that regions experiencing pushbacks record an average increase of 0.10 deaths per month, with effects concentrated in transport-related incidents rather than direct violence. I find no evidence of spatial displacement toward neighboring regions. To test the robustness of these results, I exploit Croatia’s 2023 Schengen accession as an exogenous policy shock, which coincided with a decline in both pushbacks and deaths. Overall, the findings provide causal evidence that deterrence-based border enforcement increases mortality among people on the move.
